< Mer’s Investment Advice… The Article Has Been Published. >

연합 published an article.
I had several fairly long interviews with the reporter.
The reporter asked questions so thoroughly that I ended up answering things I had never even mentioned on the blog.

The article begins like this:

There is a man everyone in Yeouido waits for every day.
No one knows his name or his face.
All they know is that he delivers posts without fail every single day, and that his pen name is “Mer.”
In the vast Yeouido district, roughly 400 times the size of a soccer field, there is hardly anyone who does not know him.

The identity of this mysterious man is that of a powerful economic blogger.
Among young securities professionals such as analysts, fund managers, and private bankers (PBs), he already enjoys “idol-level” popularity.
About 100,000 people visit his blog daily to carefully read his posts.

On the 14th, Yonhap Infomax conducted a written interview with capital market analyst “Mer.”
As the stock market surges aggressively upward, investors’ calculations are becoming increasingly complicated.
How can one move forward without losing to the market?
Yonhap sought investment advice from Mer, who is known for reading between the lines and seeing several moves ahead where others cannot.

Mer first began investing in stocks during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020.
While working at financial institutions, individual stock investments were subject to reporting requirements and restrictions, but those limitations were lifted after he moved into back-office operations.

Although his investing experience may not be long, his insight into markets is exceptional.
He worked as a risk management specialist at global companies such as Samsung Group and America’s GE, forecasting and measuring financial risks involving corporate bonds and funds.
He accumulated related experience at a total of six financial institutions.
Over the years, he reviewed and approved investments and financing related to domestic and overseas real estate investments, real estate PF non-performing loan (NPL) funds, and REITs, broadening his perspective significantly.

Mer said, “After making investment and financing decisions worth around 10 trillion won annually for nearly ten years, I developed an instinctive sense for identifying the core issues and detecting risks.” He added, “Handling investments across Korea, the United States, Australia, Singapore, Hong Kong, and the UK naturally helped me accumulate knowledge and experience.”

The KOSPI, which closed at 4,214.17 at the end of last year, has nearly doubled in just over four months this year.
On the 12th, the KOSPI briefly touched the 7,999 level during trading.
For investors, having leading stocks—or not having them—has become equally stressful.
Fear of missing out is pushing many to jump in even now, while others are weighing when to sell, worried about getting trapped at the peak.

Mer strongly emphasized, “Investors should guard against crowd psychology and stick to their own principles.”
Rather than blindly following other people’s conclusions, he stressed the need to build one’s own reasoning process.
He also advised investors to pay close attention to the moves of major institutional players such as the National Pension Service.

The following is a Q&A with Mer.

⸻

Q. When did your assets make a quantum leap, and which stock became the turning point?

The very first stock I bought after starting investing was HMM. It was during COVID-19, on October 4, 2020. I saw a news report saying that 12 HMM container ships had departed fully loaded one after another. The next morning, I immediately bought shares at 7,370 won each. I invested almost recklessly, putting everything into it, but in terms of returns, it became my best-performing investment.

I held HMM for about a year and a half before selling all of it at 26,400 won on April 2022 because I found another stock I wanted to buy. At the time, Ukraine had failed to plant crops properly due to the war, while wheat harvests in Kansas and Oklahoma were poor because of drought. In China, COVID lockdowns appeared to have caused the northeastern provinces to miss rice planting season. It all pointed toward rising food prices, so I shifted my portfolio into Japanese trading companies. Even now, Japanese trading companies account for more than 20% of my portfolio. I prefer holding investments for years rather than trading frequently.

⸻

Q. When did you move away from “all-in” investing?

I was fearless in the early stages of investing. That’s why all-in investing was possible. Until 2022, I basically just switched from HMM to Japanese trading companies. But in 2023, I started investing in silver and shipbuilding stocks as well. Whenever I achieved my target returns, I used the additional profits to buy U.S. Treasury bonds, gradually building a more diversified portfolio.

⸻

Q. Can you share your current investment allocation?

I currently keep around 50% of my portfolio in stocks. Of the remaining 50%, about 20% is invested in U.S. Treasury bonds, gold, silver, and Korean National Housing Bonds. Around 25% is held in cash, while 5% is allocated to risk assets. Within equities, Japanese trading companies and Korean shipbuilding ETFs make up the largest portion.

I began investing in gold and silver in 2023, and I have already recovered my original principal. At the moment, about 7% of my portfolio remains invested in silver. By country allocation, roughly half of my total assets are in Japan, while the other half is divided between Korea and the United States.

⸻

Q. Have you recently increased or reduced any positions significantly?

Recently, I exited all of my 10-year U.S. Treasury bond holdings when yields were in the 3.9% range. Last November, I allocated about 3% of my portfolio into three Korean copper-related stocks, reached my target returns, took profits, and recently entered again.

The 5% allocated to risk assets mainly went into Turkish and Brazilian government bonds. I also do short-term stock trading with a roughly six-month horizon.

⸻

Q. Which industries and stocks are you watching closely?

Since this year, I’ve been paying close attention to the space data center sector. Building new data centers on Earth is becoming increasingly difficult, while the cost of constructing data centers in space is rapidly declining. Although they are not yet publicly listed, I’m also very interested in private AI companies such as Anthropic and Anduril.

⸻

Q. It’s impossible to discuss global markets without mentioning AI.

People moved from OpenAI to Gemini and Claude, but these are not customers with deep loyalty. If a better feature appears, they switch very easily. It feels like a winner-takes-all war.

So much capital is being poured into this race that companies failing to gain an advantage may soon find it difficult to survive. By next year, the winners and losers in AI will probably become much clearer. If it’s difficult to predict the winners, another strategy is to invest in companies supplying products or services to them.

⸻

Q. The KOSPI is surging at incredible speed. Investors are struggling with directional bets.

You need to watch the National Pension Service closely. In the past, if stock prices rose too much, the pension fund would automatically rebalance by selling equities. Right now, however, equity exposure has far exceeded previous allocation levels, meaning they would normally need to sell unless the standards are changed.

But portfolio rebalancing has effectively been paused, and liquidity continues flowing into the stock market. The actions of these large liquidity providers matter most. For now, we need to wait and see what decision the National Pension Fund Management Committee makes.

⸻

Q. In a strong bull market like this, it’s easy to fall into the illusion that you are good at investing.

There’s something called the “Dunning-Kruger Effect.” The less capable people are, the more they tend to overestimate themselves. This stage is sometimes called the “Peak of Mount Stupid.” It’s when beginners mistakenly believe they possess expert-level knowledge and their confidence reaches its maximum.

If you ever find yourself wondering, “Why do I know so little?” that actually means you’ve already passed the Peak of Mount Stupid. It’s a positive sign that your competence is beginning to improve. On the other hand, if you’re overflowing with confidence, it may be worth asking yourself whether you are still standing on that peak.

⸻

Q. We live in an age where information is easy to access. What should individual investors be most careful about?

Listening to other people is fine. But developing the ability to arrive at your own conclusions is more important. The best method is writing. It helps to build a habit of organizing what you learned today into your own words rather than repeating someone else’s. Doing so develops the ability to distinguish valuable information from fake information.

⸻

Q. It’s not easy to focus entirely on investing. What kind of effort did you make?

Creating an “investment routine” is important. I wake up at 5:30 a.m. and start walking on the treadmill at 6. For an hour, I watch Bloomberg and CNN on the monitor, review overnight news, and think about what I need to do that day.

I usually skip lunch unless I have an important meeting. Instead, I spend that time reflecting on what happened during the morning. I also rarely schedule dinner appointments. At night, I review the events of the day and study anything I want to explore further.

On weekends, I go to the library. I borrow about 14 books per month on average. I enjoy what I call “breaking bookshelves.” I wander through library shelves regardless of subject and look for books worth reading. There’s a unique joy in unexpectedly discovering fascinating books from categories I never would have considered.

Of course, books have a weakness: they are stories of the past. That’s why combining books with up-to-date information from search engines and AI tools often leads to excellent investment ideas.

⸻

Q. Seeing the world differently, connecting unrelated things, reading between the lines, and spotting opportunities others miss—what made that possible for you?

Read a lot of books and pay attention to what’s happening overseas. At some point, a large framework naturally begins to form in your mind. Once the framework is established, adding details becomes easy.

When you understand the histories of different countries and compare timelines horizontally, you start gaining entirely new perspectives.

⸻

Q. Which book have you reread the most?

When I was young, my father bought the Japanese historical novel series Taemang and its sequel Hudaemang, totaling 35 volumes. I couldn’t fully understand them at the time, but ever since middle school, I’ve reread them dozens of times.

⸻

Q. If your account balance suddenly became zero, would you be confident in rebuilding wealth through stocks within three years?

The important thing is maintaining basic cash flow. Wealth accumulation begins only when you still have money available for investing after covering essential expenses. Salaried workers should prioritize their jobs, while business owners should focus on growing their core business to create room for investment.

Starting from zero, I don’t think it’s realistic to rebuild significant wealth through stocks within just three years. My style is more about finding strong companies and holding them patiently, aiming for something like a threefold return over three years.

⸻

Q. Do you regularly buy lottery tickets?

Not once. Paying 1,000 won for odds worth maybe 200 won never appealed to me.

⸻

Q. Do you have any hobbies that help with investing?

I enjoy looking at maps. I like static Google Maps, but I also frequently use apps like MarineTraffic, which tracks ships in real time, and FlightRadar24, which tracks aircraft.

If ships waiting to enter or leave the Panama Canal are heavily backed up for a long time, that can hint at supply shortages and future inflation.

I also recommend a free app called World Monitor. It provides near real-time updates on incidents and events happening around the world, and I use it often.

⸻

Q. Who is “Mer’s Mer”?

Warren Buffett. I first became aware of him while dealing with one of his company’s CEOs during work. Through a transaction I led, our company recovered roughly 200 billion won during the financial crisis. It may have been pocket change for Berkshire Hathaway, but for me personally, it was one of the proudest moments of my career.

My interest in Buffett began through business dealings, and the more I learned about him, the more fascinated I became. When I sold HMM and moved into Japanese trading companies, I referenced Warren Buffett’s investment moves quite heavily.

⸻

Q. You sometimes shared your daughter’s investment portfolio on your blog. What thoughts did you have watching her trust her own judgment and achieve good results?

At first, when I saw that all three stocks in my daughter’s portfolio were shipbuilding stocks, I thought, “That’s such a beginner-style all-in investment.”

But I also realized that she focused first on the shipbuilding industry itself rather than individual companies, and she held on without selling from 2023 until now, maximizing her returns. In that sense, I sometimes felt she was better than me.

At the end of last March, my daughter told me, “I sold Korea Carbon, one of my profitable shipbuilding stocks, and used the money to buy Bitcoin.” Looking at the chart now, she bought near the bottom and has already made significant unrealized gains.

Individual investors have two major advantages over institutional investors. First, they are not evaluated every year, so they can hold investments for years. Second, they can step away from investing temporarily when the market environment looks unfavorable. My daughter’s strategy of choosing industries expected to keep growing for years and investing long term made me realize she was using one of those advantages very effectively.
