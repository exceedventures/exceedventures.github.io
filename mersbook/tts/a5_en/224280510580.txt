Here’s a light health story for the weekend.

Starch is a structure made of long chains of glucose molecules and is a rich energy source found in rice, wheat, corn, and other carbohydrates. During digestion, starch is broken down into glucose, which raises blood sugar levels and is used as energy.

Starch can generally be divided into three categories depending on how quickly it is digested:
rapidly digestible starch (RDS), slowly digestible starch (SDS), and resistant starch (RS), which is not digested.

Among these, resistant starch is the key point.
Since resistant starch is not digested, it is not absorbed in the small intestine and instead reaches the large intestine. Once there, it becomes food for gut bacteria, while the remaining portion is eventually excreted as stool.

A research team at Newcastle University conducted a 20-year double-blind study related to resistant starch. A double-blind study is a method in which both the researchers and participants are unaware of the detailed conditions in order to improve the reliability of the results.

The research team gave resistant starch or a placebo daily for 29 months to 1,000 volunteers participating in the clinical trial and then followed them for 20 years. Among the 463 people who consumed resistant starch, only 5 developed upper gastrointestinal cancer, while 21 out of the 455 people who took the placebo developed cancer.

The study suggested that consistently consuming resistant starch could reduce the occurrence of cancer by more than 60%. Another point that attracted attention was that the protective effect appeared to continue for up to 10 years even after participants stopped taking resistant starch.

On May 12, 2025, research findings on resistant starch from researchers at Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine were cited by the American business magazine Fortune. The report explained that when carbohydrates such as rice or bread are cooked and then cooled, resistant starch is formed. This resistant starch may help regulate blood sugar levels and serve as food for beneficial gut bacteria.

The researchers said resistant starch may help lower cholesterol levels, reduce the risk of colon cancer, and improve gut health by feeding beneficial intestinal bacteria. As an easy way to obtain resistant starch, they recommended cooking carbohydrates and then cooling them overnight in the refrigerator.

During the cooling process, molecular bonds form resistant starch, and once it is created, much of it remains even after reheating.

Similar findings were reported in studies by other research teams as well. When freshly cooked white rice was refrigerated for 24 hours and then reheated in a microwave, it contained 2.6 times more resistant starch than freshly cooked rice.

The foods that showed even greater effects than white rice were boiled potatoes or sweet potatoes that had been refrigerated for 24 hours after cooking. However, there was an important difference with potatoes and sweet potatoes.

While resistant starch in rice remained even after reheating, researchers found that reheating potatoes or sweet potatoes reduced their resistant starch content. In other words, potatoes and sweet potatoes should be boiled and then cooled sufficiently before eating, whereas rice can still retain resistant starch even after being reheated.

One-line comment:
Convenience store lunch boxes or triangle kimbap, which are cooked in factories, distributed cold, and then reheated before eating, might not be as unhealthy as people think after all….
