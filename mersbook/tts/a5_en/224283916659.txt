Title: Not Dentures or Implants — Regrowing Teeth Instead (Update)

There have been some new developments, so here’s an update.

Humans have to rely on a single set of permanent teeth for their entire lives.
Since people live much longer than in the past, problems with permanent teeth have become increasingly common, and dental implants are now widely used.

In the past, dentures were the dominant solution.
Dentures are supported by the gums rather than the jawbone.
Because of the continuous pressure, dentures often caused gum damage and deterioration of the alveolar bone, sometimes leading to a sunken facial appearance.

Implants work differently.
Instead of relying on the gums, a screw is inserted into the jawbone, and an artificial tooth is attached on top.
Compared to dentures, implants reduce gum damage and bone loss, while also providing more than five times the chewing strength, which is why their use continues to increase.

However, inflammation remains a major problem.
Inflammation can occur around the implanted screw, damaging the gums and jawbone to the point where further implants may no longer be possible.

The root cause is that humans only grow permanent teeth once in a lifetime.
Sharks, on the other hand, have hundreds of teeth and continuously grow new ones when old teeth fall out.

Scientists studying shark teeth discovered that humans also possess tooth buds, just like sharks do.
Humans have these tooth buds, but a protein called USAG-1 prevents them from developing into actual teeth.

In 2018, researchers confirmed that suppressing the USAG-1 protein in mice caused new teeth to grow.
The same success was later achieved in ferrets and mice, both of which, like humans, have baby teeth and permanent teeth.

The next step was to test whether humans could also regrow teeth.

A research team from Kyoto University working on this technology later founded a company called Toregem Biopharma.
By September 2025, Toregem Biopharma had begun Phase 1 clinical trials at Kyoto University involving 30 men aged 30 to 64 who had partially lost teeth due to cavities or other causes.
This stage focuses on evaluating the safety and potential side effects of the treatment.

The company aims to begin Phase 2 trials in 2026 and Phase 3 trials between 2027 and 2029, with the goal of obtaining national health insurance coverage by 2030.

The chances of successfully regrowing teeth appear promising, but the key issue is whether serious side effects will emerge.

If the clinical trials succeed and the treatment becomes commercialized, this research could open an era in which people regrow natural teeth instead of relying on dentures or implants.

Toregem estimates that the treatment will initially cost around 1.5 million yen (about 14 million KRW), making it more expensive than implants or dentures at first.

Recently, additional developments have been reported.

The Phase 1 trial has reportedly progressed smoothly, with no significant side effects observed so far.
On September 29, 2025, Japan’s Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare designated Toregem’s TRG035 as an orphan drug for the treatment of “severe congenital partial anodontia,” a condition in which some teeth never develop naturally.

This designation opens the possibility of using the treatment for people born missing certain teeth.
Orphan drug status also allows priority review, which could shorten the commercialization timeline.

On November 27, 2025, Toregem announced that it had received official feedback from the U.S. FDA and was preparing to begin clinical trials in the United States.

As of May 2026, the company is continuing Phase 1 follow-up observations and plans to begin Phase 2 within the year.
Phase 2 will target children aged 2 to 7 who were born missing four or more teeth.

If Phase 2 is successful, the final Phase 3 trials will focus on people who lost teeth due to cavities or aging.

The 30 participants who received the injections during Phase 1 are reportedly continuing to regrow teeth successfully as of May 2026.
Toregem’s official Phase 1 results are expected around the fourth quarter of 2026, after follow-up monitoring is completed.

TRG-035 works by stimulating the natural regeneration of teeth with a single injection, without surgery or implants.

Although the treatment is expected to be expensive at the beginning — around 1.5 million yen — the company believes that large-scale production after commercialization could eventually lower costs to levels similar to implants.

One-line comment:
The longer people live, the more important dental health becomes. While implants and other alternatives exist, nothing truly matches natural teeth. As populations age, the silver industry is likely to continue expanding.
