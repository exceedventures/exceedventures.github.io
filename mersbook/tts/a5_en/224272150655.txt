This is a weekend health story, but it also includes topics related to pharmaceuticals.

Cancer cells are somewhat like zombies.
Cells that should die don’t die, and instead survive and rapidly multiply.
When there are only a few zombies, they can be eliminated. But once they take over a village and spread to others, the situation becomes hopeless.

Cancer can occur anywhere there are cells, but the growth rate differs.
In areas where cells grow quickly, cancer also grows rapidly.

First-generation anticancer drugs destroy all fast-growing cells, based on the characteristic of cancer cells growing quickly.
Hair follicles, which also grow quickly, are treated like cancer cells and get destroyed, which is why patients lose their hair during chemotherapy.

These first-generation drugs cannot distinguish between cancer cells and normal cells.
They attack all rapidly dividing cells and are commonly called chemotherapy drugs.

Because they attack all fast-dividing cells, side effects are severe, including hair loss, infertility, vomiting, and malnutrition.

Second-generation anticancer drugs are targeted therapies.
They attack only specific cancer cells. For example, a leukemia drug works only on leukemia, which significantly reduces side effects.

However, targeted therapies have limitations because they work only on specific types of cancer.

Third-generation drugs are called immunotherapies.
They remove the disguise of cancer cells that pretend to be normal cells, allowing the body’s immune system to attack them.

Because they use the immune system, they are effective for various cancers and have fewer side effects.
However, they are not perfect, as only about 20–40% of patients respond.

Recently, fourth-generation anticancer drugs have been developed and are being introduced.
While the first three generations rely on external substances to attack cancer cells, fourth-generation drugs use the patient’s own cells.

These are called CAR-T cells, often referred to as “serial killers of cancer cells,” because once injected, they multiply and continuously destroy cancer cells.

The “CAR” comes from “chimera” in Greek mythology, and “T” refers to T cells.
It means T cells equipped with a chimera-like cancer detection system.

The treatment involves extracting T cells from the patient, adding genes that enable them to detect and destroy cancer cells, and then reinfusing them into the body.

Once injected, the modified T cells selectively attack cancer cells and maintain their detection ability as long as the patient survives.

CAR-T therapy has shown groundbreaking results for blood cancers that previously had no effective treatment.
With a single injection, cancer cells disappear in about 67% of patients.

The downside is cost.
Since the patient’s T cells must be extracted, genetically modified over several weeks, and reinfused, the treatment can cost up to $500,000.

On April 29, 2026, the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety approved a CAR-T therapy developed by a Korean pharmaceutical company.
Insurance coverage has not yet been finalized, but it is expected after price negotiations.

Currently, a similar CAR-T therapy, Kymriah by Novartis, is available in Korea.
Its cost per treatment is about 360 million KRW, but with insurance, patients pay around 6 million KRW.

This means over 350 million KRW is covered by national health insurance per patient.

The new Korean drug is expected to be slightly cheaper than Kymriah, but it will still likely cost around 250–300 million KRW per injection.

Although it may seem like pharmaceutical companies make huge profits, reality can be different.

This treatment targets a specific cancer called DLBCL (Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma).
It involves B cells, which are part of the immune system.

Unlike typical cancers that form solid tumors, this cancer spreads diffusely throughout the body, making surgery difficult.

It is also hard to diagnose early and can lead to death within months due to rapid cell division.

In Korea, about 3,000 patients are diagnosed with DLBCL annually.
Around 60% are cured with standard chemotherapy, while about 1,200 require second-line treatment.

Second-line treatment involves autologous stem cell transplantation, which is time-consuming and physically demanding.

Patients undergo high-dose chemotherapy to eliminate cancer cells, which also destroys bone marrow.
Stored stem cells are then reinfused to restore bone marrow function.

Recovery takes months, and the process is very difficult, especially for older or less healthy patients.

If this fails, CAR-T therapy is used as a third-line treatment.
Due to mortality during earlier treatments, only about 200 patients become eligible.

Even if the price per treatment is very high, the total number of patients is small.

They also agreed on a spending cap with national insurance, limiting total reimbursement.

Another limitation is time.
CAR-T therapy requires several weeks to prepare, and some patients deteriorate before treatment.

Also, only major hospitals can manage side effects such as cytokine release syndrome and neurotoxicity.

The Korean therapy has an advantage in shorter waiting time, reducing it to about 2–3 weeks, which may allow more patients to receive treatment.

However, the total market size remains limited.

If you’ve followed the flow so far, you can see the limitations of this drug.
Although it shows a strong complete remission rate of about 67% with a single treatment, the maximum number of patients is around 200, which limits total revenue.

Complete remission means that cancer cells can no longer be detected through imaging or other diagnostic methods.
While recurrence is still possible, it indicates that the cancer has temporarily disappeared from the body.

There are several possibilities for expanding this new drug.

The first is to use CAR-T therapy as a second-line treatment instead of a third-line treatment.
Although this sounds simple, there are several obstacles.

The biggest issue is the national health insurance budget.
Since more than 300 million KRW is spent per patient, expanding coverage without limits would be a financial burden.

While third-line treatment is covered by insurance, patients who want to use it earlier may need to pay out of pocket.
If used after second-line treatment fails, patients only pay about 6 million KRW, but using it earlier could cost over 300 million KRW, which is a difficult decision for most families.

Another issue is clinical trials.
Since current data only supports third-line treatment, additional trials are required to expand its use to second-line therapy.

Even if expansion succeeds, the financial benefits may be limited.

Another approach is to expand its application beyond DLBCL to other types of blood cancer.
While this is possible given the nature of the drug, it would require additional clinical trials, which means more time and cost.

Entering the global market is another option.
However, there are already strong competitors, so it would need to demonstrate clearly differentiated efficacy.

If such differentiation is proven, one possible strategy is to license out the technology to global pharmaceutical companies.

In short, a Korean pharmaceutical company has developed a highly promising anticancer drug and proven its efficacy and safety to gain regulatory approval, but it still faces challenges in expanding its market.

From an investment perspective, having a great product and making significant profits from it are often two very different things.
After more than a decade of effort to develop this drug, it would be nice to see the company achieve meaningful financial success as well.
