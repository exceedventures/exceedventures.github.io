Since it’s Friday night, here’s something light.

On May 15, 2026, NASA’s Psyche spacecraft is scheduled to arrive near Mars and begin a gravity assist maneuver.
A gravity assist is a technique used to increase a spacecraft’s speed by using a planet’s gravity, since it cannot carry enough fuel to travel deep into space on its own.
It works like throwing a ball toward an oncoming train—the ball rebounds at high speed, gaining energy from the train’s motion.

The Psyche spacecraft was launched in October 2023 aboard SpaceX’s Falcon Heavy rocket, and its destination is the asteroid Psyche.
This asteroid, shaped somewhat like a lumpy potato and about the size of Korea’s Gyeongsang region, is located approximately 512.6 million kilometers from Earth.

Asteroids are broadly classified into three types based on their composition: C-type (carbon-rich), S-type (rocky), and M-type (metal-rich).
About 75% of asteroids are C-type, while only 38 out of roughly 1.5 million known asteroids in the solar system are M-type.

Psyche is the largest of these 38 M-type asteroids, essentially making it a massive chunk of metal.

The spacecraft is expected to arrive at Psyche in August 2029 and will orbit it for 26 months, sending various data back to Earth.
The estimated value of the metals contained in Psyche is said to be around $1 quintillion.
That amount is enough to pay off the U.S.
national debt of $34 trillion about 294,117 times.

Unlike most asteroids, which are primarily composed of rock or ice, Psyche consists largely of metals such as gold, platinum, nickel, and iron.
Scientists believe it may be the exposed core of a protoplanet that lost its outer layers (mantle) due to massive collisions during the early formation of the solar system.

By studying Psyche, scientists hope to gain insights into the composition of Earth’s core.

Of course, the mission is not just about observation and research despite its $1.2 billion cost.
Another goal is to evaluate the feasibility of in-situ resource utilization (ISRU)—mining metals directly on the asteroid and using them as construction materials for facilities such as Mars bases.

Efforts to mine resources from near-Earth asteroids are also underway separately from the Psyche mission.
In January 2022, former scientists from SpaceX, NASA, and Virgin Orbit founded AstroForge.
AstroForge aims to mine platinum-group metals such as platinum and palladium from asteroids and bring them back to Earth by 2032.

Even a relatively small M-type asteroid the size of a soccer field is said to contain about $50 billion worth of platinum-group metals.

Among various metals, platinum was chosen for its cost-effectiveness.
Its annual production is less than one-tenth that of gold, making it rare and essential for advanced industries.
While it is scarce on Earth, it is often found in much higher concentrations on asteroids.

Another reason for choosing platinum is its high melting point—1,768°C compared to gold’s 1,064°C.

The plan is to refine the mined platinum aboard a spacecraft into high-purity ingots weighing 20–30 kg, place them in capsules, and send them back to Earth.
The capsules would deploy parachutes at an altitude of 10 km, slow down, land in the ocean, and then be recovered using tracking devices.

China launched the Tianwen-2 mission on May 28, 2025, aboard a Long March 3B rocket.
This mission is heading to the near-Earth asteroid Kamoʻoalewa to collect samples and return them.

While Psyche is over 500 million kilometers away, Kamoʻoalewa is only about 15 million kilometers from Earth, making it much quicker to reach.

Tianwen-2 is expected to arrive at the asteroid in June 2026, collect samples, and send them back to Earth.
However, the spacecraft itself will not return.
After dropping the sample capsule to Earth, it will accelerate again and proceed to explore comet 311P/PANSTARRS between Mars and Jupiter as part of a “1+1” mission.

AstroForge, the leading private asteroid mining company in the United States, has faced repeated failures.
Its first spacecraft, launched in April 2023, failed to conduct in-space refining experiments due to communication issues.
Its second spacecraft, launched in February 2025, also lost contact shortly after launch due to a technical malfunction.

A third spacecraft is planned for launch in the second half of 2026, with the goal of actually landing on an asteroid.

AstroForge operates under the motto “fail fast, fail often,” launching spacecraft annually and continuing its efforts despite setbacks.

One-line comment:
In the U.S., private companies are leading asteroid resource development, while in China, the government is taking the lead.
It may not be the immediate future, but by the 2030s, we might start seeing precious metals mined from asteroids.
Since industries like this can evolve unpredictably, it’s worth keeping an eye on them from time to time.
