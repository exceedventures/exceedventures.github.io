Because this topic could have significant repercussions, I’ve organized the key points below.

Sam Altman was born into a wealthy Jewish family and studied computer science at Stanford University.
In 2005, at age 19, he founded the social networking app Loopt and sold it seven years later in 2012 for $43.4 million.
In 2014, Altman became CEO of Y Combinator and hit a jackpot with investments like Airbnb, pushing valuations beyond $65 billion.
In 2015, Altman left Y Combinator and co-founded an AI research organization with Elon Musk.
That organization is OpenAI, the creator of ChatGPT.
Altman, politically aligned with Democrats, clashed with Musk (who leans Republican) over fundraising and political activities.
Musk argued there was a conflict of interest since Tesla was also developing AI, and he left OpenAI’s board and cut funding.

OpenAI had an unusual structure: a nonprofit (OpenAI Inc.) controlling a for-profit entity (OpenAI LP). Investors could earn up to 100x returns, with excess profits going back to the nonprofit.
Microsoft invested heavily, securing exclusive rights to GPT-3 and later acquiring a 49% stake in the for-profit arm.
A key concept is AGI (Artificial General Intelligence), meaning AI that can perform any intellectual task like a human—unlike current “weak AI” such as ChatGPT or DALL-E.
If ChatGPT reaches AGI, it could perform complex financial activities like stock trading, options, and futures.
OpenAI originally aimed to protect humanity from runaway AI. Its board determines whether AGI has been reached, and if so, commercial activities must stop—affecting partnerships like Microsoft.
The 2023 firing of Altman was linked to AGI concerns. The board emphasized preventing harmful or power-concentrating AI, implying Altman was pushing commercialization too far.
Ilya Sutskever, OpenAI’s chief scientist and former Google researcher (worked on AlphaGo), is considered a “Messi-level” talent in AI.
He led an AGI safety (alignment) team to control AI beyond human capability. Alignment refers to ensuring AI follows human intentions.
Conflict escalated when Altman reduced the alignment team’s scope. Sutskever later said the board acted to ensure AGI benefits humanity.

In a 2023 interview with The New York Times, Altman said his goal was to generate vast global wealth and redistribute it, and to build good AI before bad actors do.
This suggests the conflict was about how AGI should be used, not just a power struggle.
After Altman’s firing, over 500 of 800 employees supported him, leading to his return as CEO. Opposing figures like Sutskever later left.
Another group opposing commercialization left and founded Anthropic, focusing on safe, controllable AI.
As of April 28, 2026, a legal battle between Musk and Altman began in federal court in Oakland.
Musk claimed he proposed merging OpenAI with Tesla under his control but was rejected, leading to his departure.
Altman argued Microsoft investment was necessary after Musk withdrew funding. Musk claims OpenAI abandoned its nonprofit mission and became a closed subsidiary of Microsoft.
Musk criticized OpenAI, alleging it hides AGI-level capabilities like GPT-4.
Under agreements, Microsoft’s license would end if AGI is reached—creating incentives to deny reaching AGI.

Altman dismissed Musk as a “sore loser.” Musk demands returning $134 billion to the nonprofit and removing Altman and Greg Brockman.
Witnesses like Shivon Zilis and Sutskever may reveal more details.
Zilis, a longtime Musk ally, is also known for having children with him, reflecting Musk’s belief in addressing population decline.
By 2026, OpenAI’s structure changed significantly—removing profit caps and AGI-related restrictions, making IPO easier.
The board is now fully aligned with Altman, consolidating his control.

One-line comment
In 2026, IPOs are being pursued by OpenAI, SpaceX, and Anthropic—all linked to this lawsuit. OpenAI appears most at risk; delays could strain cash flow in a market with limited capital and many mega-IPOs. Musk may also be strategically hindering a competitor.
