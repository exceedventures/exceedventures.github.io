Title: Collaboration Between Elon Musk and Samsung Electronics? The Story of Neuralink

This is an update to a topic I’ve been following for the past two years.
Recently, there have been noticeable developments, so I decided to organize my thoughts.

In 2016, Elon Musk founded Neuralink together with eight scientists and engineers.
The company’s goal was to develop brain–computer interface (BCI) technology that directly connects the human brain to computers.
Because the technology involves implanting chips into the brain, it is often referred to as a “brain implant.”

The first objective of the brain implant was to develop a method that would allow people suffering from paralysis caused by spinal cord injuries or strokes—people whose minds remain intact but whose bodies cannot move—to communicate.

The technology works by implanting electrodes thinner than a human hair into the motor cortex to capture electrical signals and transmit them externally to control devices.

Neurons, which make up the brain, transmit data through electrical signals.
These signals generate wave patterns, and if electrodes are implanted close enough to detect those waves, brain activity can be interpreted.

In theory, if electrodes are placed within 0.006 centimeters of neurons, it becomes possible to capture their signals.

Because the human brain contains enormous numbers of neurons, many electrodes are required.
Neuralink currently uses 1,024 electrodes. Since manually implanting that many electrodes is impractical, dedicated robots perform the procedure.

Elon Musk has stated that robots were necessary because humans could not achieve the speed and precision required for the operation.
He has also claimed that robots will soon surpass excellent surgeons and could outperform the best surgeons within five years.

During human procedures, the robot scans the brain using AI and successfully implants electrodes while avoiding blood vessels.

Musk has compared the surgery to LASIK in terms of simplicity and has claimed there are no lasting side effects.

However, even if the implantation process itself is streamlined, the surgery is not inherently simple.
The procedure requires a craniotomy—creating a circular opening in the skull.

A coin-sized section of the skull is removed, electrodes are inserted through the opening, and a wireless communication module is installed on top of the skull.

Initially, the external unit was attached near the ear, but later designs improved this by embedding the module within the skull and restoring the scalp over it.

Once covered by the scalp, it becomes difficult for outsiders to tell whether the person has undergone the procedure.

Charging is performed wirelessly, similar to an Apple Watch.

Neuralink introduced this product under the name “Telepathy.”

In May 2023, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved clinical trials involving implantation of Telepathy in patients with paralysis.

Neuralink recruited participants and, eight months later, completed implantation in the first human subject.

After surgery, Telepathy functioned successfully, transmitting neural signals to the Neuralink app, which began decoding them.

If electrical brain signals can be received and interpreted, controlling electronic devices becomes possible.

On January 28, 2024, Elon Musk announced on social media that the first patient had received a brain chip implant and was recovering well.

The first patient was a 29-year-old quadriplegic man paralyzed below the shoulders after a diving accident.

As of 2026, he reportedly continues using Telepathy for around ten hours a day to control computers, play games, and manage schedules.

In interviews, he explained that after about a year of use, his control ability improved and operating Telepathy became more comfortable.

The second Neuralink recipient was a fully paralyzed patient named Alex.

Alex demonstrated himself playing Counter-Strike 2.
He was not merely observing the game—he moved, aimed, and shot like a typical gamer.

Alex was discharged one day after surgery and reportedly began controlling the cursor within minutes of connecting the chip to a computer.

On August 22, 2024, Musk stated that he hoped millions of people would receive Neuralink chips within the next decade.

The third patient, Smith, had ALS and had lost both mobility and speech. Through AI voice functionality, he regained the ability to communicate.

The technology aims to help patients with ALS, stroke, spinal injuries, cerebral palsy, and other conditions that make communication difficult.

The speech restoration device received FDA Breakthrough Device designation, which accelerates development and regulatory review of advanced medical technologies.

Musk announced a goal of conducting roughly 30 additional implant procedures during 2025.

China is also rapidly advancing in brain implant technology.

The Chinese Institute for Brain Research (CIBR) and state-owned NewCyber NeuroTech jointly developed Beinao No.1, which completed its third human implantation in March 2025.

NewCyber NeuroTech reportedly implanted three patients in March 2025 and planned a 50-person study in 2026.

China is also exploring implantation in healthy individuals to allow control of robots and drones through thought alone, suggesting possible military applications.

Musk has expressed plans to combine Neuralink technology with Tesla’s humanoid robot Optimus.

By early 2026, 21 implant procedures had reportedly been completed—behind Musk’s announced schedule, in typical “Musk Time” fashion.

So far, no major adverse events have been reported.

One patient experienced reduced neural signal collection, but the issue was resolved through a software update without additional surgery.

A patient implanted in January 2026 regained the ability to speak, and Musk shared video evidence on March 31.

Rather than using an AI-generated voice, the system restored previously recorded speech so the patient could speak using their original voice.

The product that enables device control through thought is Telepathy, while the speech-focused product is expected to be commercialized under the name Voice.

One of Neuralink’s co-founders and current president is Dongjin Seo.

Born in Korea and raised in the United States from age four, he reportedly became interested in neuroscience after seeing his grandparents experience dementia.

Initially serving as Vice President overseeing implant development, he later became president and remains the only founding member besides Musk.

Seo earned a bachelor’s degree in electrical engineering from Caltech in 2011 and a Ph.D. from UC Berkeley in 2016 before joining Neuralink.

During graduate research, he developed wireless ultrasonic sensors small enough for use inside the body and was selected for MIT Technology Review’s TR35 Innovators Under 35.

The current N1 implant (The Link) includes 1,024 electrodes distributed across 64 threads and is coin-sized with wireless charging.

Musk outlined goals to increase electrode counts to 3,000 in 2026, 10,000 in 2027, and 25,000 in 2028.

On December 31, 2025, Musk declared 2026 to be the year of “mass production and fully automated surgery.”

Robotic systems would perform operations while eight specialized tomography cameras map brain tissue in 3D and compensate for movement caused by heartbeat and other factors.

Musk’s stated target is more than 1,000 implants during 2026.

He also outlined a long-term vision to expand beyond patients into consumer applications for healthy individuals beginning around 2030.

Reports suggest that Samsung Electronics’ foundry division has been selected as a supplier for Neuralink’s fourth-generation chips.

Samsung is reportedly developing Neuralink’s fourth-generation implant chips using a 4nm process beginning in late 2025.

Trial production reportedly started in May 2026, with mass production expected in 2027.

Neuralink’s first through third generations collaborated with TSMC, while Samsung appears to be joining for the fourth generation.

Until now, Neuralink’s N1 chips have mainly been one-way systems that read signals from the brain and send commands to devices.

Samsung’s fourth-generation chip is expected to introduce writing capability—sending information back into the brain and enabling two-way communication.

If successful, this could potentially help restore vision to blind patients under a future product called Blindsight.

The reason for using 4nm rather than leading-edge 2nm technology is reportedly reliability rather than peak performance.

Because the implant becomes a permanent medical device inside the skull, safety takes priority, and 4nm is considered sufficient to process thousands to tens of thousands of electrodes.

Samsung Foundry also secured a $16.5 billion contract from Tesla to manufacture next-generation AI6 chips at its Taylor, Texas facility.

Together with plans to manufacture xAI Grok-related processors for NVIDIA platform deployment, Neuralink would become another area of collaboration.

Although Neuralink chip supply alone may not dramatically impact revenue, the continued expansion of cooperation between Elon Musk and Samsung Electronics appears meaningful.

One-line comment

Early reactions to Telepathy were highly skeptical—many people dismissed it with comments like, “Elon Musk talks a lot, but most of it is nonsense.”
“Musk Time” often means schedules slip beyond initial targets, but progress has continued regardless.
Samsung Electronics’ foundry division, which has struggled financially, also seems to be gradually regaining momentum.
