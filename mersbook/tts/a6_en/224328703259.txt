Title: Tesla and Chicken

A light topic for the holiday.
In 1900, Korea was known as the Land of the Morning Calm.
During the same period, more electric cars than gasoline cars were driving through the streets of New York, and even sixty electric taxis were in operation.
This was the era of the first generation of electric vehicles.
At the 1900 Paris Exposition, the electric car Lohner-Porsche appeared.
Lohner-Porsche introduced an in-wheel motor, placing electric motors inside the wheels themselves.
At that time, the main problem with electric cars was their short driving range.
Early electric vehicles could travel only about 12 kilometers on a single charge, but Lohner-Porsche extended that to around 50 kilometers by installing a large battery.
Dr. Porsche continued improving the vehicle.
He created a hybrid car by adding a gasoline engine to support the electric drivetrain and produced an electric vehicle capable of traveling 200 kilometers.
Eventually, this led to the world’s first hybrid prototype called “Semper Vivus,” meaning “always alive” in Latin.
Back then, gasoline-powered cars were less competitive than electric vehicles.
To start a gasoline car, the driver had to manually crank a rotating handle with force.
For wealthy car owners, it was both inconvenient and not exactly elegant.
It seemed electric cars, which required no manual starting, would become the future of passenger transportation.
But a powerful competitor appeared.
That competitor was Ford’s Model T gasoline car.
Many people think the Model T conquered the market simply because of low prices enabled by mass production.
That is not wrong, but there was another advantage.
Ford adopted the electric starter, first developed by Cadillac.
Once automatic ignition became available, drivers no longer needed to crank engines by hand.
The 1920s also marked the period when large-scale oil drilling began in West Texas.
Gasoline became extremely cheap.
With longer driving range and easier operation, gasoline vehicles took over the market.
That is why the first generation of electric vehicles disappeared in America.
In the 1990s, California experienced rapid population growth, and air pollution became a major social issue.
California introduced regulations requiring that a certain portion of vehicles sold must produce zero carbon emissions.
At the time, virtually no such vehicles existed, making the policy highly ambitious.
General Motors invested one billion dollars into developing the two-seat electric vehicle EV1.
But EV1 had limited range, and gasoline cost only about $1.30 per gallon, so sales never truly took off.
In 2003, engineer JB Straubel and Elon Musk had lunch at a seafood restaurant in Los Angeles.
At that time, Musk already had fame and wealth as the founder of PayPal.
Straubel proposed an unmanned hydrogen-fuel electric aircraft.
Musk interrupted him and said electric aircraft lacked potential and simply did not interest him.
Running out of ideas, Straubel mentioned something he had been thinking about.
Instead of one large battery:
“What if we connected thousands of small lithium batteries—the kind used in mobile phones—and put them into an electric car?”
Musk immediately became excited.
People say this was the first time the famously frugal Musk paid for lunch himself.
That was also when he began funding the idea.
Straubel was a genius.
At age thirteen, he demonstrated his talent by upgrading the performance of an electric golf cart.
When he entered Stanford University, he designed his own major called Energy Systems Engineering, and the university created the department.
He became its only student.
After graduation, Straubel spent his time thinking about solar energy and electric vehicles until meeting Musk and finding his opportunity.
Lithium-ion batteries had already been commercialized by Exxon in the mid-1970s.
By the early 1990s, Sony had begun supplying batteries suitable for portable computers and phones.
Straubel commercialized the idea of connecting thousands of small lithium batteries into one system with Musk’s support.
In 2006, Tesla unveiled two prototype two-seat sports cars.
They were called the Roadster.
The Roadster also reflected Musk’s practical thinking.
Because it only had two seats, the space where rear seats would normally go could be packed tightly with batteries.
Anyway, it proved that lithium-ion batteries could power automobiles.
Investment began flowing in, and Tesla’s growth started.
You might wonder why this article is titled Chicken when it has only talked about electric vehicles.
Before the 1960s, chicken in Korea was commonly eaten as baeksuk (boiled chicken soup) or dakgaejang (spicy chicken soup).
Then American broiler breeds arrived in Korea.
Broiler chickens reached 1.5 kg in only 30 days, while native chickens required seven months to reach similar weight.
Chicken dishes suddenly became economical.
In 1961, Myeongdong Yeongyang Center opened in central Seoul.
The restaurant installed rotating electric rotisserie machines visible through large glass windows, displaying dozens of chickens spinning on metal skewers.
The sight of golden-brown roasting chickens with dripping fat and the rich aroma became one of Myeongdong’s landmarks.
It was the first moment many Koreans discovered the taste of roasted whole chicken instead of “chicken soaked in water.”
The restaurant became known as one of the finest places in Seoul, where fashionable people dressed in suits and dresses went on dates or arranged meetings.
They also say the tradition of serving roasted chicken in yellow paper bags started there.
Back then, whole chicken in yellow paper bags was a luxury that fathers might bring home only on payday.
In 1971, large-scale production of Haitai cooking oil began.
Then in 1977, Lim’s Chicken, opened in the basement of Shinsegae Department Store in Myeongdong, introduced the method of cutting chicken into smaller pieces and deep-frying them in cooking oil.
That became the beginning of franchise fried chicken.
Just as electric vehicles disappeared when cheap gasoline became widely available, roasted whole chicken gradually gave way to fried chicken.
Yet Yeongyang Center survived in Myeongdong.
The reason the restaurant carries the name Yeongyang (“nutrition”) is that its signature dish was originally samgyetang served in stone pots.
Today, Myeongdong Yeongyang Center has relocated and expanded over time and operates several directly managed branches, but the original flagship location remains.
Its signature thin and crispy rotisserie skin and clean, mild flavor are still preserved.

Usually, I eat chicken from Bodram Chicken or Duldul Fried Chicken, but for electric rotisserie chicken I usually get takeout from Samwoo Chicken Center near Sindaebang Station.
Myeongdong is a bit too far.
