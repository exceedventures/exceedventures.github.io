Title: The Current Situation in the World’s Worst-Performing Stock Market of 2026

If we reveal the country with the worst stock market return first, the answer is Indonesia.
Here is why.
In October 2024, Prabowo Subianto took office as president and launched an economic policy framework often referred to as “Prabowonomics.”
A former military officer, Prabowo promoted an ambitious target of 8% annual economic growth and introduced his flagship campaign promise: a nationwide free school meal program.
The plan promised free meals to 96 million people, including elementary, middle, and high school students, infants, and pregnant women, with implementation beginning with students.
Because poverty remains widespread and many students often skipped lunch, the free meal program initially appeared to be a meaningful social policy.
The policy gained strong public support. By June 2025, Prabowo’s approval rating had climbed to 81%.
In practice, Indonesia allocated $16 billion in the 2026 budget alone to provide free meals to 62.5 million students.
The problem was simple:
“Where would the money come from?”
Indonesia did not have that kind of fiscal capacity, and increasing spending would normally require raising tax revenue.
However, instead of collecting more taxes, Prabowo even canceled a previously approved VAT increase.
Indonesia also has a fiscal deficit ceiling written into law.
The government is legally prohibited from allowing the fiscal deficit to exceed 3% of GDP.
Calculations showed that introducing free meals while canceling the VAT increase would push the fiscal deficit to around 3.1% of GDP.
Facing the possibility of breaking the legal limit, Prabowo cut government spending by approximately 27 trillion won worth of budget reductions, equal to about 8.4% of total government spending.
Most of the cuts affected local governments.
The butterfly effect of those budget cuts appeared in July.
Property tax bills arrived.
In some regions, property taxes increased by an average of five times, while certain areas saw increases of up to ten times.
In Indonesia, property tax authority belongs not to the central government but to local governments.
After the central government sharply reduced transfers, some local governments facing budget shortages raised property tax rates by as much as five times in one move.
That was not all.
Authorities also updated official land values, which are used to calculate property taxes.
As a result, taxes increased sharply, and because assessed property values also nearly doubled, some households ended up facing property tax bills as much as ten times higher.
At the exact moment property owners were becoming furious over tax notices, another controversy exploded: housing allowances for members of parliament.
Indonesia consists of 17,508 islands.
Because of the country’s geography, regional lawmakers cannot easily gather in parliament the way Korean lawmakers can travel quickly by high-speed rail.
Parliament traditionally provided official residences, but many of the apartment buildings constructed in the 1980s had become outdated and insufficient.
Lawmakers who could not move into official residences began receiving 50 million rupiah per month (about 4.22 million KRW) as housing support.
The justification was that lawmakers should live near parliament to participate in meetings efficiently, so premium-level rental support was approved.
Initially, only lawmakers not living in official residences received the allowance.
However, beginning in September 2024, the benefit expanded to all 580 members of parliament.
Reports also surfaced that some lawmakers continued living in government housing while receiving housing allowances simultaneously.
As these stories spread through TikTok, public anger intensified.
Adding the housing allowance to base salary meant some lawmakers were receiving nearly 100 million rupiah (about 8.5 million KRW) per month.
Meanwhile, Indonesia’s average monthly worker income stood at only 2.84 million rupiah (about 240,000 KRW).
A parliament member’s daily earnings exceeded an average citizen’s monthly income.
This became one of the major factors behind nationwide demonstrations centered in Jakarta.
Housing support for lawmakers triggered public outrage, but at its core, the protests were driven by taxpayers angry over property taxes.
Even after promises to suspend lawmakers’ housing benefits, protests continued.
Only after postponing the property tax increase did tensions begin to ease.
Prabowo then shifted policy direction.
He began arguing that “to achieve growth, Indonesia must become bolder with debt,” signaling efforts to revise the law limiting fiscal deficits to 3%.
In other words, when the goal could not be reached, the government started changing the rules.
Legally, Indonesia must keep its national fiscal deficit below 3% of GDP.
One of the biggest reasons foreign investors had favored Indonesia was its reputation for strict fiscal discipline.
Once the government signaled that it would break the 3% rule and expand spending aggressively, foreign capital began leaving the stock market.
In 2026 alone, foreign investors recorded net selling of 70 trillion rupiah (approximately $4 billion) in Indonesian equities.
While stock markets in many countries rose during the first half of 2026, Indonesia’s market dropped 36%, making it the worst-performing stock market globally.
As foreign money flowed out, the currency also weakened sharply.
Foreign investors faced both falling stock prices and currency losses, accelerating capital flight.
On June 10, 2026, the Indonesian government unexpectedly raised gasoline prices by more than 30%, from 1,063 KRW per liter to 1,393 KRW per liter.
Although still cheaper than Korea, motorcycles are a core transportation tool for ordinary Indonesians, making such an increase feel like a direct hit to daily life.
As a result, protests that had temporarily faded began to reappear, led by university students.
Thousands marched in Jakarta chanting:
“Heading to Bankrupt Indonesia.”
As funding for the free meal program became increasingly strained, discussions recently emerged about temporarily suspending free meals during school vacations.
At the same time, Indonesia has been focusing less on labor-intensive manufacturing industries such as textiles and more on resource extraction and processing industries, especially nickel mining.
However, these industries create fewer jobs than many expect.
As manufacturing sectors that traditionally created large numbers of jobs moved to countries such as Malaysia and raw materials became the economic priority, employment opportunities declined.
Every year, approximately 1.6 million vocational high school graduates enter the labor market, but many struggle to find stable employment.
Youth unemployment exceeding 15% has also made university protests spread more easily.
Another source of concern is Prabowo’s political background—he was once both the son-in-law and a close ally of Suharto, who ruled Indonesia as an authoritarian leader for roughly 30 years—raising concerns among some people about a possible return to authoritarian governance.

Always ask:
“Where is the money coming from?”
