There are reports that Iran is considering allowing passage through the Strait of Hormuz for oil traded in Chinese yuan, so I’ve put together some background context. As always, history tends to repeat itself.
In the early stages of World War II, the United States was a neutral country and was regarded as the safest place to store gold. At the time, the U.S. operated under the gold standard, exchanging one ounce of gold for 35 dollars, meaning that deposited gold could be converted into dollars.
Countries such as the United Kingdom, Belgium, and the Netherlands moved their gold reserves to the United States to prevent them from being seized by Nazi Germany and to use them for trade. During World War II, European countries—including the UK—purchased weapons and food from the U.S., paying with the gold they had deposited.
Following the California Gold Rush (1848–1855), gold production in the U.S. increased significantly, boosting its own reserves. By the end of the war, about 70% of the world’s gold was concentrated in the United States, allowing the gold standard to function without major issues.
However, the key limitation of the gold standard was that gold supply was finite, meaning dollars could not be printed freely. If dollar liquidity doubled while gold reserves remained unchanged, either the price of gold would have to double or the value of the dollar would have to be halved to maintain balance. But since the gold standard fixed the exchange rate at 35 dollars per ounce, such market adjustments could not occur.
If too many dollars were printed relative to gold reserves, the U.S. would not have enough gold to exchange for dollars upon demand, effectively leading to default.
The Vietnam War led to massive military expenditures. As allies observed the U.S. printing large amounts of dollars without an increase in gold reserves, doubts arose about whether dollars could still be converted into gold.
Countries such as France, Spain, and the United Kingdom began bringing large amounts of dollars to the U.S., demanding gold in exchange. When even close ally Britain joined in, President Richard Nixon made the following announcement on August 15, 1971:
“The dollar is under attack by speculators. Therefore, I am temporarily suspending the convertibility of the dollar into gold.”
This marked the abandonment of the gold standard and became known as the “Nixon Shock.”
After the Nixon Shock, the price of gold quickly rose from 35 dollars per ounce to 120 dollars. In other words, the value of the U.S. dollar fell to about one-quarter of its previous level. A weaker dollar meant higher import prices, leading to rising inflation in the United States.
In November 1973, U.S. Secretary of State Henry Kissinger visited Saudi King Faisal. An agreement was reached: the U.S. would guarantee the security of the Saudi monarchy, and Saudi Arabia would sell oil exclusively in U.S. dollars.
At the time, Saudi Arabia faced threats from Israel and Iran, leaving it little choice but to accept. From that point on, the dollar became the sole currency for oil transactions—the “petrodollar”—and regained its strength.
Oil-producing countries that received dollars from oil sales used their surplus dollars to purchase U.S. Treasury bonds. This created a cycle: dollars printed by the U.S. were used to buy oil, and oil exporters reinvested those dollars back into U.S. debt.
In the same year, 1973, the U.S. created SWIFT, a system that enabled banks to transfer money across borders. Oil transactions were conducted in dollars through SWIFT, which eventually connected over 12,000 financial institutions in more than 200 countries. This is why SWIFT is often seen as having originated alongside the petrodollar system.
The issue then shifts to Iran.
In 1901, British entrepreneur William Knox D’Arcy secured a 60-year concession to develop oil in Iran and soon discovered massive reserves. In 1909, he established the Anglo-Persian Oil Company (APOC).
Winston Churchill, then First Lord of the Admiralty, recognized oil’s strategic importance and arranged for the British government to acquire a 51% stake in APOC. This company later became British Petroleum (BP).
At the time, Britain paid Iran a 16% royalty on oil profits. However, after World War II, Iran demanded renegotiation. As U.S. oil companies agreed to a 50-50 profit-sharing arrangement with Venezuela in 1949 and Saudi Arabia in 1950, Iran’s demands grew stronger.
The U.S., with greater financial flexibility, accepted the 50-50 split and offset the impact on oil companies by reducing corporate taxes. Britain, lacking such fiscal capacity, could not do the same and needed to maximize its revenues from Iran.
Amid ongoing negotiations in October 1949, Mohammad Mossadegh—who campaigned on oil nationalization—was elected Prime Minister. He escalated demands from a 50-50 split to full nationalization and implemented it in April 1951.
In June 1951, British warships headed toward Iran, preparing for military intervention. At the time, the U.S. was entering the Cold War with the Soviet Union, and Iran was both an oil producer and a strategic location.
The U.S. feared that if Iran fell under communist influence, the Soviet Union could gain control over the Middle East, which accounted for two-thirds of global oil production. However, the U.S. was not prepared to engage in a full-scale war with the Soviet Union at that time.
Since the U.S. military buildup would not be sufficient until late 1952, policymakers concluded that no actions should provoke a major conflict with the Soviet Union before then (based on recently declassified documents).
In this context, Iran nationalized its oil industry.
Britain attempted to reverse nationalization through military force, but the U.S. sought a negotiated solution by mediating between the two sides. However, Mossadegh remained firm, and negotiations failed.
On September 12, 1951, Mossadegh ordered all British employees of APOC to leave Iran within three days. Britain responded by blocking Iran’s oil exports and imposing a naval blockade.
In February 1952, the British navy seized an Iranian oil tanker en route to Italy near Yemen, effectively cutting off Iran’s oil trade. As a result, Iran’s oil revenue plummeted from $400 million in 1950 to just $1 million in 1951.
That same month, Britain severed diplomatic relations with Iran, froze Iranian assets, and imposed export bans on goods such as sugar and steel.
Mossadegh appealed to the United Nations, but his request was rejected. Meanwhile, Churchill devised a plan to deploy 70,000 troops to seize Iran’s oil fields and refineries.
In response to U.S. President Harry Truman’s opposition to military action, Churchill argued:
“The United States should support Britain’s military action in Iran in return for Britain’s support in the Korean War. Iran is more important than Korea. Securing stable oil supplies through Iran is essential to deterring Soviet aggression.”
By the summer of 1952, the U.S. began to view Mossadegh’s uncompromising stance as an obstacle to resolution. The focus shifted from negotiation to removing Mossadegh from power.
On November 20, 1952, the White House approved the NSC-136/1 plan to carry out a joint covert operation with Britain to remove Mossadegh. The operation was jointly conducted by British intelligence and the CIA.
This effort, known as Operation Ajax, led to regime change in Iran, with results emerging in August 1953.
